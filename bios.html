2013-2014 Cultural Heritage Informatics Grad Fellow Bios

Ethan Watrall

An archaeologist who has worked extensively throughout North America and the Near East, Ethan Watrall is an Assistant Professor in the Department of Anthropology and Associate Director of MATRIX: The Center for Digital Humanities & Social Sciences (http://matrix.msu.edu) at Michigan State University. In addition, Ethan is Director of the Cultural Heritage Informatics Initiative (http://chi.anthropology.msu.edu) and the Cultural Heritage Informatics Fieldschool at Michigan State University (http://chi.anthropology.msu.edu/fieldschool/). Ethan’s primary interests lie in the domain of digital cultural heritage and digital archaeology, with particular focus on serious games & meaningful play, mobile & geospatial (mostly within the context of public outreach and engagement), and linked open archaeological data. Ethan is PI of the recently completed, NEH funded "Red Land/Black Land: Teaching Ancient Egyptian History Through Game-Based Learning” project. Ethan is founder and editor of Play the Past (http://www.playthepast.org), a collaborative authored, scholarly blog dedicated to exploring the intersection between games and cultural heritage. In addition, Ethan is co-editor of Archaeology 2.0: New Tools for Communication and Collaboration, an open access volume published by the UCLA Cotsen Institute of Archaeology Press (http://www.escholarship.org/uc/item/1r6137tb). In addition to his scholarly work, Ethan has written trade books on web and interactive design, including Head First Web Design published by O’Reilly.

Brian Geyer

Brian Geyer is a graduate student in the sociocultural anthropology program at Michigan State University (MSU) and a Foreign Languages and Area Studies (FLAS) Fellow in Michigan State University's African Studies Center. He earned his first Bachelor's degree at Washington State University in Music in 2008, with a second in Anthropology in 2009. Prior to attending Michigan State, Brian served as a public health Peace Corps Volunteer in Kenya, where he learned his love for indigenous studies while living among a rural Maasai population. Upon completion of his doctorate, he hopes to join a research university faculty and continue his research on topics of power structures, inequality, and ecotourism.

Katy Meyers

Meyers is a PhD graduate student in Archaeology who is studying the relationship between inhumation and cremation burial practices, specifically focusing on Anglo-Saxons. She also is the creator and author of Bones Don't Lie

, a mortuary archaeology blog, and a co-founder and editor for GradHacker, a collaborative blog for graduate students. Meyers also is the Assistant Editor for Bioarchaeology of the Society for Archaeological Sciences Bulletin. Prior to this year, Meyers has been the Campus Archaeologist for MSU, and a teaching assistant within the Anthropology Department. Beyond Anthropology, she is the Recording Secretary for the Council of Graduate Students and teaches social media workshops to MSU graduate students. Meyers is a strong advocate for Linked Open Data and digital methods in Archaeology, and this summer she was selected as a fellow for the Linked Ancient World Data Institute. She is excited to be re-joining the Cultural Heritage Informatics Iniative program as a returning fellow, and looks forward to developing new digital projects.

David S. Bennett

My area of study is the unique border where society and technology touch. While this largely puts me in the shoes of the a cultural and political historian, I focus primarily on technology’s role in those spheres. At this stage of my study, I am measuring the impact television has had on society during the late 1950s and early 1960s. My dissertation in particular will focus on the public’s reception of the civil rights movement television news coverage. In addition to this research, I was a teaching assistant in the 2012-2013 school year, and am currently a research assistant for the Vietnam Archive Project, which is funded by the National Endowment of the Humanities.

While my research will hopefully serve to help us understand the various ways technology changes society, my research also helps me build a more interactive pedagogical style. My goal in the classroom is to help students engage in historical study with the same focus and scope as students engage in the physical world. To accomplish this successfully, I create my lectures to stimulate as many of the student senses as possible. I not only present an engaging narrative, but also force students to experience the guiding influences that stemmed from and affected those events. Every stage of the classroom experience would be a learning opportunity that stimulates all segments of the student’s senses; I structure every test to give students feedback so that students are consistently adding material to their interpretive framework. My returned tests report personalized error structures for the student to work on in order to improve for the next test. My critical analysis rubric outlines generalized errors while giving the student direction on how to improve their argumentation skills. In lectures, I utilize contemporary digital and physical media, historical and news documents, visual art, music, and televisual footage, to assist students in experiencing a visceral history then allotted to them through texts.

I have taught classes on Early American History, Late American History, Early American Intellectual and Cultural History, and The Francophone World. I have also assisted in the following courses on Late World Civilizations, Integrated Studies in the Arts and Humanities focused on US and the World, and Integrated Social Studies course on Time, Space, and Change in Latin America.

My favorite recreational activity is playing board games.
